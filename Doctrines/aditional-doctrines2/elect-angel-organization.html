Elect Angel Organization

  1. Introduction
    1. The conclusion of the prehistoric angelic conflict in heaven yielded two opposing forces: those angelic creatures who were elect (who chose for God) and those who were fallen (who chose against God and followed the fallen). The true size of these angelic armies is indeterminable but likely in the hundreds of millions. Of the elect angels, one-third of the total angelic force, God said thousands upon thousands (Ps. 68:17a); and in Gethsemane Jesus said, “Or do you think that I cannot appeal to My Father, and He will at once put at My disposal more than twelve legions of angels?” (Matt. 26:53). The Apostle John “heard the voice of many angels around the throne … and the number of them was myriads of myriads, and thousands of thousands” (Rev. 5:11).
    2. These myriads of elect angels in the heavenly protocol are a well-defined organization. Created to glorify God, they perform various duties in heaven and on Earth, heralding and executing His plans. They are involved in the creation, prophecy, declarations and judgments, battling the demonic hordes as combatants in God’s heavenly army, and ministering to believers on Earth.
  2. Ranks and Description
    1. Seraphim (שְׂרָפִים seraphim) are the super-royal/seraphic elect angels. “Each had six wings: with two he covered his face, and with two he covered his feet, and with two he flew” (Isa. 6:2). “Seraph” means “burning ones.” They are described in Isaiah 6:2, “Seraphim stood above Him, each having six wings: with two he covered his face, and with two he covered his feet, and with two he flew.” Seraphim are specifically associated with the throne of God in Isaiah 6.
    2. Cherubim (כְּרוּבִים kerubim): the royal elect angels. God has “made them ride on a cherub and fly; and He sped upon the wings of the wind” (Ps. 18:10). Cherubim guard the throne of God, the garden of Eden, and the ark of the covenant. They are commanded by Jesus Christ, “the Lord of hosts” (Ps. 46:7), and composed of three descending ranks: the six-winged seraphim, who lead in worship and humility in God’s holiness; the four-winged cherubim, who guard God’s holiness; and the wingless elect angels, who serve as messengers, guardians, and soldiers.
    3. There is much we do not know about elect angels. God has not revealed many of their functions and hierarchy primarily in the Books of Daniel, Isaiah, Zechariah, Luke, and Revelation.
  3. Seraphim have two roles:
    1. As leaders of heavenly worship, to glorify God.
    2. As angelic heralds, to function as messengers between God and man.
  4. Highest Ranking Seraphim—Archangels
    1. Comparable to four-star generals, archangels perform the most critical and distinguished duties with regard to divine revelation and judgment.
      1. Michael
        1. The title of archangel is specifically used with Michael in Jude 9 and 1 Thessalonians 4:16 with the devil and Michael contending over Moses’ body.
        2. Michael commands the army of angels who defend Israel.
  5. The four cherubim, one at each wheel of the chariot of fire (Ezek. 1; 10).
  6. Lucifer, described as “the anointed cherub” (Ezek. 28:14–17).
  7. Of the twenty-four elders (angels?) seated on twenty-four thrones surrounding the throne of the Lord Jesus Christ (Rev. 4:4). These cherubim, “clothed in white garments,” are honored and decorated with robes and golden crowns. They are closest to the throne of God and are in direct subordination to His authority, the twenty-four remove their crowns and present them to the Lord (Rev. 4:10).
  8. The angel of Revelation 7:2, who issues a restraining order to the four angels of Revelation 7:1.
  9. The angel with the “golden censer [shovel]” (Rev. 8:3).
  10. The “seven peals of thunder,” teachers of eschatology (Rev. 10:3–4).
  11. The “strong angel” in midheaven in charge of all evangelism in the Tribulation (Rev. 14:6).
  12. The angel who announces the fall of “Babylon the great [ecumenical religion in the Tribulation]” (Rev. 14:8).
  13. “Another angel, a third one,” who warns of worshipping the beast and his image, and of receiving the mark on the forehead or upon his hand (Rev. 14:9). The “wrath of God” awaits those who convert to ecumenical religion during the Tribulation (Rev. 14:10).
  14. The angel who announces the fall of religious judgment in the last half of the Tribulation (Rev. 14:17–19).
  15. Detonation and Description
    1. Wingless angels make up the majority of the angelic armies. Their description and execution have wings as badges of rank.
    2. Wingless angels perform three general missions as messengers of judgment, as guardians, and as soldiers.
  16. Wingless Angels—Angels in Scripture
    1. The two angels who visited Abraham with Lot and then destroyed Sodom (Gen. 19:1–25).
    2. The destroying angel of 2 Samuel 24:16.
    3. The “band of destroying angels” of Psalm 78:49.
  17. The “execrationers of the city” and the “man clothed in linen” in Ezekiel 9:1–11.
  18. The four angels who restrain “the four winds of the earth” (Rev. 7:1).
  19. The “seven angels who had the seven trumpets” (Rev. 8:6).
  20. “Seven angels who had seven plagues” (Rev. 15:1).
  21. Guardian angels (Ps. 91:7–14).
    1. Psalm 91:11–12 explains the function of guardian angels: “For He will give His angels charge concerning you, To guard you in all your ways. They will bear you up in their hands, Lest you strike your foot against a stone.”
    2. There are three categories of guardian angels.
      1. The guardian angel of children. All children have a guardian angel to protect them during childhood (Matt. 18:10).
      2. Guardians of all Church Age believers (Heb. 1:14).
      3. Custodian angels of client nations (Dan. 10:13, 20).
  22. The Heavenly Host
    1. The remaining non-distinguished, non-ranking, rank-and-file elect angels comprise the army, an organization of angelic soldiers (1 Kings 22:19; 2 Chron. 18:18; Ps. 148:2; Luke 2:13; Rev. 5:11). These are the “host of heaven” or “armies” of heaven and then lead the elect angels against the demon armies.
  23. Gabriel
    1. Gabriel was commissioned to explain to Daniel the vision of the ram and he-goat in Daniel 8:16 and the prediction of the seventy-week eschatology in Daniel 9:21–27.
    2. He was sent to Zechariah to announce the birth of John the Baptist (Luke 1:11–19).
    3. He was sent to the virgin Mary to announce the birth of Christ (Luke 1:26–38).
    4. Called “an angel of the Lord” in Luke 2:9, he brought the news of the First Advent to the shepherds.
    5. He is “the archangel” whose voice command will resurrect Church Age believers at the Rapture (1 Thess. 4:16–17).
    6. He is the angel who will chain Satan in his hand, who ordained Isaiah (Isa. 6:6–7).
    7. The “four living creatures” stationed in the throne room of God (Rev. 4:6–8) who proclaim the judgment of historical disaster (military invasion, economic collapse, national, natural, disease) of the Tribulation (Rev. 6–18).
    8. The “strong angel” of Revelation 5:2; 10:1; and 18:21.
    9. The messenger sent from God the Father to God the Son in Revelation 14:15. This angel takes messages from God the Father in the “temple which is in heaven” (Rev. 15:5–8) to the Lord Jesus Christ in heaven prior to His tribulational judgment on Earth.
    10. The angel with “power [authority] over fire” who “came out from the altar” (Rev. 14:18).
